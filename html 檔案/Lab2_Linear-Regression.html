Example 2:¶

In this problem we will investigate the t-statistic for the null hypothesis $H_0:\beta_1 = 0$ and $H_0:\beta_2 = 0$ in multiple linear regression. To begin, we generate predictors $x1,\ x2$ and a response $y$ as follows.¶

set.seed(1)¶

x1=rnorm(100)¶

x2=rnorm(100)¶

y=5+2*x1+3*x2+rnorm (100)¶

(a.) Write out the form of the multiple linear model.¶

(b.) Create a scatterplot displaying the relationship between $x2$ and $y$. Comment on what you observe.¶

(c.) Perform a multiple linear regression of $y$ onto $x1$ and $x2$. Report the coefficient estimate, the standard error of this coefficient estimate, and the t-statistic and p-value associated with the null hypothesis $H_0:\beta_1 = 0$ and $H_0:\beta_2 = 0$.¶

Comment on these results. (You can use the command by lm(y∼x1+x2) .)¶

Solution 1:¶

(a.)¶

In [6]:
set.seed(1) #r
x1=rnorm(100) #100(N(0,1))
x2=rnorm(100)
y=5+2*x1+3*x2+rnorm (100) #

The form of the multiple linear model is $y=5+2\times x1+3\times x2+\epsilon$, and $\epsilon\sim N(0,1)$.¶

(b.)¶

In [7]:
plot(x2, y)

The relation between $x2$ and $y$ looks linear and with a positive slope.¶

According to the summaary result above, we have：¶

$\hat{\beta}_0=5.0254$, $\hat{\beta}_1=2.0211$, and $\hat{\beta}_2=2.9465$.¶

$se(\hat{\beta}_0)=5.0254$, t-statistic is $47.77$ and the p-value is less than $2\times10^{-16}$.¶

$se(\hat{\beta}_1)=2.0211$, t-statistic is $17.31$ and the p-value is less than $2\times10^{-16}$.¶

$se(\hat{\beta}_2)=2.9465$, t-statistic is $26.91$ and the p-value is less than $2\times10^{-16}$.¶

Under $\alpha=0.05$, since their p-value are very low we can reject $H_0:\beta_1 = 0$ and $H_0:\beta_2 = 0$ so that $x1$ and $x2$ are highly significant.¶
